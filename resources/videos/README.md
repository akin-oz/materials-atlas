# Videos

This index is for high-value long-form learning resources.

Videos are included when they provide orientation, technical depth, or research context that is difficult to recover from isolated papers. Short promotional clips, conference advertisements, and scattered tutorials do not belong here.

## How To Use These Videos

Videos should not replace primary sources. Use them to understand relationships:

- Orientation videos help place domains in the field.
- Tutorial videos connect software to workflows.
- Research talks connect people, institutions, papers, and open problems.
- AI-for-materials talks should be read after the materials informatics and graph-model canon, not before it.

## Orientation

### NIST AIMS Workshops

Why it belongs: The NIST Artificial Intelligence for Materials Science workshops provide field-level orientation across AI, materials data, standards, uncertainty, and scientific workflows. They are useful for seeing how government, academic, and industrial perspectives intersect.

Best use: Watch selectively to understand the structure of the AI-for-materials conversation and the practical barriers to adoption.

Related domains: AI for Materials, Materials Informatics, Research Infrastructure.

Leads to: FAIR principles, uncertainty, standards, and reproducible AI-for-materials practice.

### Materials Project Tutorials

Why it belongs: The Materials Project is a central infrastructure project for computed materials data. Its tutorials help learners understand how high-throughput computed data is accessed, interpreted, and connected to practical research workflows.

Best use: Use when learning how computational materials databases become working research tools.

Related domains: Materials Informatics, Density Functional Theory, Research Infrastructure.

Leads to: Materials Project, pymatgen, high-throughput DFT, and database-driven discovery.

### Materials Cloud Tutorials

Why it belongs: Materials Cloud connects computational materials data, provenance, workflows, and reproducibility. Its tutorials are useful for understanding research infrastructure rather than only end-user data access.

Best use: Use when studying reproducible computational materials workflows and curated simulation data.

Related domains: Research Infrastructure, Computational Materials.

Leads to: provenance, workflow publication, NOMAD, and AiiDA-style reproducibility questions.

## Foundations

### MIT and Markus Buehler Talks

Why it belongs: Markus Buehler's talks connect materials modeling, mechanics, multiscale thinking, and modern AI directions. They are useful for understanding how computational materials ideas extend from atomistic structure to larger-scale behavior and design.

Best use: Watch for conceptual orientation, especially when connecting materials science, mechanics, simulation, and machine learning.

Related domains: Computational Materials, Molecular Dynamics, AI for Materials.

Leads to: multiscale modeling, learned structure-property relationships, and AI-assisted materials design.

### Berkeley Materials Seminars

Why it belongs: Berkeley seminars expose learners to active research culture around materials computation, materials discovery, databases, and adjacent experimental work.

Best use: Use as research-context material after the foundations are in place.

Related domains: Computational Materials, Materials Informatics.

Leads to: current research questions and institutional context.

## Scientific Software

### Materials Project Developer and API Tutorials

Why it belongs: These tutorials show how scientific software, databases, APIs, and workflows connect in practice. They are particularly useful for software engineers entering the field.

Best use: Use when learning how to build against computational materials infrastructure rather than only consume static data.

Related domains: Scientific Computing, Materials Informatics, Research Infrastructure.

Leads to: pymatgen, Materials Project APIs, workflow automation, and reproducible analysis.

### Materials Cloud Workflow Tutorials

Why it belongs: Workflow and provenance are central to reproducibility. Materials Cloud tutorials help learners understand how calculations, metadata, and published results are connected.

Best use: Use when studying reproducible computation and workflow-centered research.

Related domains: Research Infrastructure, Computational Materials.

Leads to: AiiDA, provenance, Materials Cloud archives, and FAIR computational data.

## AI for Materials

### Microsoft AI for Science Talks

Why it belongs: Microsoft AI for Science is one of the major industrial research efforts connecting machine learning, simulation, scientific foundation models, and materials discovery.

Best use: Watch to understand how industrial AI research frames scientific modeling, data, scale, and deployment.

Related domains: AI for Materials, Research Infrastructure.

Read after: Materials Informatics, machine learning foundations, and graph neural network papers.

### Google DeepMind Talks on GNoME and MatterGen

Why it belongs: GNoME and MatterGen are important examples of large-scale AI systems applied to materials discovery and generation. They should be studied as research claims with assumptions, validation choices, and deployment limits, not as slogans.

Best use: Watch alongside the corresponding papers and independent discussion of benchmarks, synthesis feasibility, and validation.

Related domains: AI for Materials, Materials Informatics.

Read after: CGCNN, MEGNet, M3GNet, CHGNet, and high-throughput materials data.

## Research Talks

### Argonne National Laboratory Seminars

Why it belongs: Argonne is a major center for materials research, computation, characterization, and large-scale scientific infrastructure. Its seminars help learners see how computational methods connect to national-lab research programs and facilities.

Best use: Use to understand research context, experimental-computational links, and applied scientific infrastructure.

Related domains: Computational Materials, Research Infrastructure.

Leads to: HPC awareness, national-lab workflows, characterization-computation links.

### Berkeley Research Seminars

Why it belongs: Berkeley is central to several computational materials and materials informatics efforts, including the Materials Project ecosystem. Its seminars provide context around active research, tools, and scientific questions.

Best use: Use for topic scouting after a learner has enough background to follow primary research talks.

Related domains: Materials Informatics, Computational Materials, AI for Materials.

Leads to: Materials Project ecosystem, Berkeley research groups, and current domain questions.
