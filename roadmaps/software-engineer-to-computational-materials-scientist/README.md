# Software Engineer → Computational Materials Scientist

> A practical, research-oriented curriculum for experienced software engineers transitioning into Computational Materials Science.

---

# Purpose

This curriculum is the execution layer of Materials Atlas.

Materials Atlas explains the field.

This curriculum explains what to study next.

It is designed for experienced software engineers who want to build genuine competence in computational materials science without repeating an entire undergraduate degree.

The objective is not breadth.

The objective is research readiness.

---

# Target Learner

This curriculum assumes the learner:

- holds a bachelor's degree in Metallurgical and Materials Engineering (or an equivalent discipline)
- has forgotten much of the undergraduate theory
- has several years of professional software engineering experience
- is comfortable with programming and modern software engineering practices
- wants to transition into computational materials research

It is **not** intended for first-year university students.

---

# Design Principles

This curriculum follows a few simple rules.

## Learn only what unlocks the next capability.

Topics exist because they enable future work.

Nothing is included simply because it appears in a traditional university curriculum.

---

## Theory follows necessity.

Mathematics, physics, and chemistry are introduced only when they become necessary.

For example:

- Statistical Mechanics appears before Molecular Dynamics.
- Quantum Mechanics appears before Density Functional Theory.
- HPC appears when simulations become computationally meaningful.

---

## Build while learning.

Every module produces artifacts.

Examples include:

- Jupyter notebooks
- Python programs
- simulation outputs
- reproduced figures
- paper summaries
- mini research projects

Learning without producing artifacts is considered incomplete.

---

## Prefer depth over coverage.

One excellent textbook is better than five mediocre ones.

One canonical lecture series is better than dozens of unrelated videos.

One reproduced paper is better than reading twenty abstracts.

# Completion Criteria

This curriculum is complete when the learner can:

- understand modern computational materials literature
- reproduce published computational results
- contribute to open-source scientific software
- design computational experiments
- critically evaluate simulation results
- communicate computational materials research clearly
- begin graduate-level computational materials research with confidence

---

# Study Philosophy

Consistency is more important than intensity.

A sustainable pace over many months is preferred to short bursts of activity.

The curriculum should produce lasting understanding rather than temporary familiarity.

---

# Estimated Duration

Approximate duration:

**12–18 months**

assuming **10–15 focused hours per week**.

Experienced learners may progress faster by demonstrating the exit criteria of each module instead of spending fixed amounts of time.

---

# Progression Rule

Do not advance because enough time has passed.

Advance only after satisfying the exit criteria of the current module.

The curriculum is competency-based rather than time-based.

---
