# Domain Name

Use this template for every domain page.

Write guidance and curation, not an encyclopedia article. A domain page should help readers understand what the domain is, why it matters, what comes before it, and where it leads.

## Overview

Define the domain in plain language.

Explain what question, method, material class, modeling scale, or research program the domain covers. Keep the scope explicit enough that future additions can be judged against it.

## Why It Matters

Explain the scientific or engineering importance of the domain.

Focus on what the domain makes possible, what problems it addresses, and why a computational materials researcher should understand it.

## Historical Evolution

Describe the durable historical arc of the domain.

Emphasize conceptual transitions, not exhaustive chronology. Show how current methods emerged from earlier ideas.

## Current State

Summarize how the domain is practiced today.

Identify what is mature, what is actively changing, and what claims require caution.

## Prerequisites

State what the reader should understand before studying this domain.

Use the relationship vocabulary from [EDITORIAL.md](../EDITORIAL.md).

## Core Concepts

List and explain the concepts required to navigate the domain.

Prefer concepts that help readers interpret papers, software, datasets, or research claims.

## Learning Objectives

Describe what the reader should be able to explain, reproduce, implement, evaluate, or modify after studying the domain.

Use outcomes only when they improve clarity.

## Canonical Books

Include only books that provide durable foundation or reference value for the domain.

For each book, explain why it belongs, when to read it, and what gap remains without it.

## Canonical Papers

Include papers that define the domain, change its direction, explain a method, introduce infrastructure, or clarify evaluation.

Do not create a long bibliography. Explain the role of each paper.

## Scientific Software

Identify software that embodies the domain in practice.

Distinguish simulation engines, scientific libraries, workflow systems, and data platforms when relevant.

## Datasets

Identify datasets or databases that are trusted, canonical, or necessary for understanding the domain.

Explain provenance, use cases, and limitations.

## Leading Researchers

Name researchers only when their work helps readers understand the domain.

Explain influence, methods, infrastructure, or intellectual lineage. Do not write biographies.

## Leading Laboratories

Name laboratories, institutions, or groups only when they clarify where important work happens.

Explain their role in methods, data, software, facilities, or research programs.

## Industrial Adoption

Explain where and how the domain is used outside academic research.

Avoid promotional language. Focus on durable adoption patterns and practical constraints.

## Open Problems

Identify unresolved scientific, computational, data, software, or adoption problems.

Open problems should help readers understand what remains uncertain.

## Related Domains

List nearby domains and explain the relationship.

Use this section to prevent isolated pages.

## Read After

State which concepts, roadmap stages, or domains should come before this one.

This section should help a newcomer avoid learning the domain too early.

## Continue With

State what becomes possible after understanding this domain.

Point to next domains, software layers, research practices, or specialization paths.
